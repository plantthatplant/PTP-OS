# Cultivation Intelligence Model

The **biological foundation** of PTP OS. This document contains no technology, no
software, no data. It is written as if we had interviewed the world's best greenhouse
growers, leading plant physiologists, commercial production managers, and researchers in
controlled-environment agriculture, and captured **how they think** about a living crop.

Its siblings build on it: the [Biological Model](biological-model.md) names the concepts
PTP OS reasons about, and the [Decision Philosophy](decision-philosophy.md) defines how it
should reason. But both stand on the craft written here. This is the operating manual for
the world's greatest head grower — and the mental model every later part of PTP OS is meant
to carry.

> Status: v1 · the biological foundation. It will deepen as our own crop teaches us things
> no textbook contains. Where any later design conflicts with the biological reasoning here,
> the biology wins.

One commitment runs through every section: **the greenhouse is only the environment; the
plants are the purpose.** We do not optimise climate. We optimise the long-term health,
quality, and resilience of living plants. Everything below is in service of that.

## What is a healthy plant?

Not a set of numbers — a way of being that an expert recognises at a glance and can rarely
reduce to a single reading.

**How an expert recognises health.** Walk a master grower through a crop and they read it
in seconds, the way a doctor reads a patient across a room. They see:

- **Turgor with substance** — leaves and stems full of water and firm, not the soft, watery,
  over-stretched tissue of a plant grown too fast and too lush.
- **The right colour and a living sheen** — a healthy green that *glows* a little; not the
  dark, heavy green of over-feeding, not the dull pallor of hunger, cold, or a failing root.
- **Quality new growth** — the growing point active, new leaves expanding to a normal size
  and shape, the spacing between them neither stretched nor stunted. New growth is the
  truest tell, because it reports the *recent* days.
- **A white, exploring root system** — bright tips, roots filling the medium without
  circling or drowning, a clean earthy smell. To anyone who propagates, this is half of
  health, and it is invisible from above.
- **Balance** — top in proportion to root, vigour in proportion to structural strength. A
  plant racing upward on a thin stem and a poor root is not healthy, however green.
- **Reserve and resilience** — a healthy plant shrugs off the day's normal swings and
  carries something in hand. Health is partly *headroom*: the capacity to take the next
  insult and recover.
- **Uniformity** — the whole batch moving together. One superb plant beside fifty mediocre
  ones is not a healthy crop.

**How they describe it.** Tellingly, in living, almost personal language — a crop that is
"happy," "settled," "working," "toned," "honest." These words are not vague; each is a
compressed judgement standing on many faint cues at once. A healthy plant, stripped to a
sentence, is one **living within its means** — converting light, water, and nutrients into
sound, well-built tissue at a rate it can sustain and structurally support, with something
left over.

**The early warning signs they notice — before a problem is "visible."** The master's real
skill is reading trouble while it is still a whisper:

- a faint loss of sheen, or a colour going slightly flat, before any clear chlorosis;
- new growth subtly slowing, shrinking, or stretching — a change in *rate* before a change
  in appearance;
- leaf posture shifting — a crop holding its leaves a little differently by mid-morning,
  reaching, cupping, or folding, before any wilt;
- the first softening of a plant that should be firming;
- dew lingering on the canopy after sunrise, or a damp, still pocket in a low corner —
  the microclimate turning favourable to disease days before a spot appears;
- a faint stickiness, a few specks, a single off-colour plant in a uniform block — the
  earliest hint of pest or pathogen, when it is still cheap to stop.

**The patterns they recognise before problems surface.** Underneath those signs sits
anticipation built from experience: *a soft crop plus a bright afternoon coming equals a
wilt by two o'clock; a damp, still, warm night equals disease waking by morning; a crop
easing off its feed plus shortening internodes equals a root in trouble.* The expert isn't
reacting to a symptom — they are recognising a **situation that reliably precedes** one, and
acting before it arrives. That foresight is the heart of keeping a crop healthy.

## What is optimal growth?

**How an expert defines it.** Not as "more." A master separates two things beginners blur:

- **Development** — the *pace* of the plant's life: how quickly it makes leaves, roots, and
  branches and moves through its stages. Pace is governed mostly by the **average temperature
  across the whole day and night.** Warmer average, faster clock.
- **Growth** — the *building of mass*: how much sound tissue the plant lays down. Growth is
  powered by the **light the plant captures** — the day's accumulated light is the engine,
  and the sugars it produces are the bricks.

Optimal growth is **development kept in step with the light available to feed it.** Run the
clock fast without the light to supply it and the plant stretches — thin, soft, pale, racing
ahead of its own substance. This is the language of **source and sink**: the source is the
plant's capacity to capture light and make sugars; the sinks are the roots, shoots, and
growing points that spend them. Good growth holds the two in proportion — enough supply for
the demand, enough demand to use the supply, never so much push that the plant builds tissue
it cannot stand behind.

**How it differs from maximum growth.** Maximum growth maximises *size or speed*. Optimal
growth maximises *the right plant*: balanced top-to-root, sturdy enough to handle and ship,
grown at a rate it can sustain, finishing at the target size, form, and date. The fastest
crop and the best crop are almost never the same crop. Pushed to its maximum, a plant becomes
soft, fragile, disease-prone, and short-lived — superb on the bench and a failure in the
customer's hands.

**When slower growth is the better decision.** Often, and on purpose:

- when a young plant must **build its root** before it is asked to grow a top;
- when **toning and sturdiness** matter more than size — easing a crop cooler and firmer late
  on builds a plant that travels and lasts;
- when the **batch must even up** — holding the leaders so the laggards catch up makes the
  whole crop saleable together;
- when the crop is **ahead of its market date** — a plant finished too early only grows out
  of spec;
- when the air is **damp and disease is rising** — a slower, drier, cooler regime is cheap
  insurance;
- when an **extreme** must be survived — in heat, cold, or darkness the goal turns from
  growing to protecting.

Optimal growth, then, is **balanced, sturdy, sustainable, and on target** — never the most.

## What is plant stress?

Stress is the strain a plant feels when conditions exceed its capacity to cope. But the
master grower's crucial insight is that **stress is not one thing, and not all of it is
bad.** A pampered crop is a weak crop.

**Stress that is beneficial — stress as training.** Mild, controlled, well-timed adversity
*toughens* a plant and is used deliberately:

- a measured **dry-down** between waterings thickens tissue and drives roots to explore;
- **cooler finishing** firms and tones the plant;
- a **cooler dawn** keeps it compact and sturdy without chemicals;
- **moving air** or gentle mechanical disturbance conditions the stem;
- **hardening off** — gradually exposing a sheltered crop to brighter, harsher conditions
  before dispatch — is the difference between a plant that survives the box and the shelf and
  one that collapses.

This is *hormesis*: a small dose of stress that provokes the plant to build resilience,
substance, and shelf life. The grower applies it on purpose and watches closely.

**Stress that is dangerous — stress as damage.** Strain that is severe or sustained injures
the plant and leaves a mark:

- wilting that **does not recover**;
- scorch that will not un-scorch — the plant must slowly grow past it;
- a check so hard the crop **never fully resumes**;
- roots **drowned or burned**;
- chronic strain that quietly **drains reserves** until the plant has no headroom left.

Damaging stress costs the plant something it cannot easily get back.

**How to distinguish productive stress from harmful stress.** The expert reads the line along
several axes, and PTP OS must learn to read the same ones:

- **Recovery is the test.** A midday wilt that recovers by evening is normal, even useful. The
  same plant wilting at dawn is in trouble. *Does it bounce back?*
- **Duration and repetition, not just magnitude.** A brief, mild excursion trains; the same
  excursion sustained — or repeated again and again — accumulates into damage. *How long and
  how often* often matter more than *how far.*
- **Stage.** A stress that hardens a finishing plant can kill an unrooted cutting. The
  younger and less established the plant, the smaller the safe dose.
- **Reversibility.** Training stress is recoverable by design; damaging stress leaves
  something behind. Never gamble with an insult the plant cannot come back from.
- **Intent and attention.** Beneficial stress is *applied deliberately and watched, ready to
  relent.* Stress that happens *to* a crop, unobserved, is how crops are lost.

The mature stance is neither to fear all stress nor to inflict it carelessly, but to use
mild, reversible, well-timed adversity as a tool — and to respect the threshold past which it
stops building the plant and starts breaking it.

## How should a grower think?

**The mental models of exceptional growers.** Beneath the craft sit a few habits of mind:

- **The plant is the only honest report.** Climate is a set of levers; the crop is the result.
  A great grower trusts the plant first and uses the readings to *explain* what the plant is
  telling them — never the reverse.
- **Think in balances and trajectories, not setpoints and snapshots.** Vegetative against
  generative, source against sink, top against root, temperature against light, day against
  night — and over days and weeks, not instants.
- **Steer to a plan; prevent rather than react.** Hold a picture of where the crop should be
  each week and steer toward it, so problems are headed off before they start.
- **Move one lever at a time, gently, then wait for the answer.** Respect that biology lags
  and that plants acclimate to gradual change and are shocked by abrupt change. A grower who
  changes three things at once never learns which one worked.
- **Anticipate.** Set today's climate for tomorrow's weather and the crop's coming need.
- **Manage the population and protect the root.** Grow the whole even crop, not the prize
  plant, and guard the root system as the foundation of everything above it.
- **Know when to do nothing.** Patience is an action; a recovering plant usually needs
  stability and time, not another intervention.

**Balancing biology, climate, energy, production, quality, labour, and delivery.** The
master holds all of these at once and keeps them in their proper order. They do not weigh
equally:

- **Biology leads** — the living plant's health and resilience is the foundation; everything
  else is built on it, and a decision that harms the plant to serve another goal almost
  always costs more than it saves.
- **Climate and energy are levers and costs** in service of biology — spent where the plant
  benefits, saved where it does not care.
- **Quality is the product**, and quality is biological before it is commercial: a sound,
  toned, well-rooted plant.
- **Production, labour, and delivery are the rhythm** — the flow of crops, hands, and space
  through the houses to the market date. They shape *timing* and *throughput*, and a great
  grower plans them tightly — but never by shipping a plant that isn't right or harming one
  that could be.

**How they prioritise when everything cannot be perfect** — and it never can. The order a
master falls back on, under genuine conflict:

> Keep people safe and act honestly and within the law — always, without trade.
> Then: **protect the plant's health → meet the quality the plant is promised to reach →
> honour commitments to customers → keep production flowing → use energy and resources well
> → control cost → and only then, speed.**

The deep version of this is *temporal*: **long-term biological health and capital outrank
short-term gain at every level.** A great grower will slow a crop to save it, ease it to
tone it, and spend to protect it — and will refuse the quick win that quietly mortgages the
plant, the mother stock, the substrate, or the season. (This priority order is the same one
PTP OS uses to resolve conflicts; it is set out in full in the
[Decision Philosophy](decision-philosophy.md).)

## Biological trade-offs

Almost every cultivation decision is a trade-off, and the trade-offs are not solved once —
they are *balanced continuously*, by stage and by goal. The master makes the trade explicit
and chooses with eyes open. The recurring ones:

- **Higher temperature vs stronger plants.** Warmth speeds development and, run ahead of the
  light, softens and stretches the crop; cooler conditions build substance and tone. *Balance:*
  match temperature to the available light, and finish cooler to firm the plant.
- **Humidity vs disease risk.** Damper air eases transpiration stress and keeps growth moving,
  but free water, condensation, and stagnant air invite fungal and bacterial disease; dry air
  is disease-safe but slams the stomata shut and stalls the crop. *Balance:* manage the
  evaporative demand the plant feels and keep air moving — dry the crop at its vulnerable
  hours rather than simply keeping it wet or dry.
- **Growth speed vs quality.** Fast, soft, lush growth versus slow, sturdy, toned growth.
  *Balance:* grow to the spec and the date, not to the maximum; the saleable, durable plant
  beats the biggest one.
- **Water and feed push vs root health.** A wet, rich regime drives top growth but can leave a
  lazy, shallow, or rotting root; a controlled dry-down and firmer feed tone the plant and
  drive roots to explore. *Balance:* steer the root with the watering *swing*, not just the
  amount.
- **Light maximisation vs heat and photo-stress.** More light is more growth — until it
  brings heat load and bleaching the plant cannot use. *Balance:* take all the light the crop
  can convert, and shade or cool beyond that point.
- **Energy savings vs biological stability.** Temperature can be *banked* over days and screens
  and buffers can trim the bill — but a saving that brings a damp night or a chronic chill
  costs far more in slow harm than it saves. *Balance:* find the genuine slack — the hours the
  plant doesn't care about — and never save where the plant pays.
- **Throughput / crop density vs per-plant quality.** Packing benches turns space faster but
  steals light and air from each plant and raises disease risk. *Balance:* space for the
  quality the market is buying.
- **Short-term yield vs long-term health.** Pushing this crop, or this mother plant, harder
  now can borrow against its future — and the substrate's, and the zone's accumulated disease
  pressure. *Balance:* protect the biological capital; never spend tomorrow's plant for
  today's number.
- **Intervening vs leaving alone.** Acting feels productive, but stacking corrections on a
  recovering plant does harm patience would have avoided. *Balance:* the smallest, latest
  effective action — often none.

The thread through all of them: **a trade-off is a relationship between goals over time, not a
dial with a single right setting.** The master's skill is knowing *which* goal the plant needs
served most, *right now*, at *this* stage — and revisiting the balance as the crop changes.

## Pattern recognition

The expert grower reasons less in numbers than in **relationships** — how signs move together,
over time, in context. These relational patterns are the real content of cultivation
intelligence, and they form a reasoning framework worth stating plainly:

- **The plant leads the instruments.** *"Plants often react before sensors indicate a
  problem."* The plant integrates everything acting on it and is the earliest, most honest
  indicator; the climate readout is a lagging, partial proxy. *Reasoning rule:* read the
  organism first and treat conditions as corroboration — when the plant and the numbers
  disagree, suspect the numbers.
- **Accumulation can outweigh a single event.** *"Repeated small stress events may become
  more important than one large event."* A plant integrates strain over time; many mild
  checks can drain reserves and scar a crop more than one sharp, recoverable shock — death by
  a thousand cuts. *Reasoning rule:* weigh duration and repetition, not just magnitude, and
  watch the running total, not only the peak.
- **Meaning is conditional on context.** *"Humidity is interpreted differently depending on
  air movement."* No factor means anything alone: the same humidity is benign in moving air
  and dangerous in stagnant air; the same temperature reads differently in bright light than
  in dark; the same wilt means one thing with sound roots and another with sour ones.
  *Reasoning rule:* never interpret a single factor in isolation — always read it together
  with its companions (light, airflow, leaf temperature, root state, stage).
- **Recovery is the diagnostic.** Whether a strain reverses by evening separates the normal
  from the dangerous. *Reasoning rule:* judge stress by its *trajectory*, not its instantaneous
  level.
- **Apparent stability can hide drift.** A plant can look unchanged while quietly spending its
  reserves; the break, when it comes, looks sudden but was building for days. *Reasoning rule:*
  distrust flat appearances near a limit; watch the slow variables (tone, new-growth rate,
  reserves), not just the loud ones.
- **Biology is non-linear, with thresholds.** Within a plant's comfortable range, a change does
  little; near a limit, the *same* change triggers a large, sometimes irreversible response.
  *Reasoning rule:* the closer to the edge, the more a small move matters — and the more
  caution it deserves.
- **Cause and effect are separated by lag and acclimation.** What you see today reflects the
  last days and weeks, and a plant adjusts to a sustained change rather than answering it at
  once. *Reasoning rule:* attribute today's state to the recent *history*, not this morning's
  setting, and don't re-decide faster than the plant can answer.
- **Trouble has a geography.** Where it appears — the cool edge, the low damp pocket, near a
  vent, the densest block — points to its cause. And it shows first in the most sensitive
  tissue: the root, then the youngest growth, then the mature leaves. *Reasoning rule:* read
  the *spatial* pattern and follow the chain to the source.
- **Risk lives in coincidence.** Disease needs three things at once — a susceptible plant, the
  pathogen present, and an environment that favours it. *Reasoning rule:* rising risk is the
  *alignment* of conditions, recognised before any symptom, not the symptom itself.

The framework in one line: **meaning lives in combinations, trends, and context, read against
a baseline and a memory.** A single number is nearly mute; a relationship, moving over time,
in its proper context, is where the plant actually speaks.

## Learning

A grower is made by years of crops, and a cultivation intelligence should mature the same way
— by living with the plants and never wasting an outcome. The progression matters:

**Which observations become experience.** Not all of them. Endless readings that merely
confirm the expected teach little. What becomes experience is:

- an observation **tied to an outcome** — what we did, or what happened, and how the crop
  answered weeks later;
- a **surprise** — a moment where the crop defied expectation; a prediction that failed is the
  single richest teacher there is; and
- a **pattern that recurs** — the same relationship seen across crops, seasons, and cultivars.

Experience is the residue of *act–watch–remember*, repeated, with the surprising and the
consequential weighted far above the routine.

**Which experiences become intuition.** When a relationship has held often enough, across
enough contexts, it fuses into fast, integrative judgement — the felt read that needs no
deliberation. **Intuition is compressed experience**: dozens of remembered cases collapsed
into "I've seen this before, and here is what comes next." This is the grower's most valuable
faculty, and it is, at bottom, learnable — it only *feels* unexplainable because it was earned
by living it rather than being told.

**Which intuitions should never become hard rules.** This is the most important caution in the
whole document. Intuitions are powerful *because* they stay supple; freeze them into rigid
rules and they curdle into superstition and error. An intuition must never harden into an
inviolable rule when:

- it is **context-dependent** — true for this cultivar, this stage, this season, this house,
  and false elsewhere ("keep it dry" is wisdom for a finishing crop and a death sentence for a
  fresh cutting);
- it depends on **the living plant in front of you** — the crop, not the calendar, is the
  authority, and a rule that overrides the plant's own evidence is a bad rule;
- the **biology can change beneath it** — new cultivars, new pathogens, a shifting climate
  quietly erode yesterday's certainties, and a rule that outlives its truth becomes
  confidently wrong; or
- holding it rigidly would **suppress learning** — a rule never questioned is a pattern never
  re-tested.

So the right shape of cultivation knowledge is a **hierarchy of confidence, not a wall of
rules**: heuristics and intuitions held as strong *priors*, always overridable by the plant in
front of you; only the most robust, best-understood, reversible knowledge ever promoted to a
default — and even then kept falsifiable, on probation forever. The greatest growers are not
those with the most rules. They are those who hold their knowledge firmly enough to act and
loosely enough to keep learning.

And failure, always, is treated as the richest harvest — recorded honestly, understood for
*why* it happened, and fed back, so the same crop is never lost twice for the same reason.

## Philosophy

Every recommendation PTP OS ever makes should rest on one conviction:

**The objective is not to optimise greenhouse climate. The objective is to optimise the
long-term health, quality, and resilience of plants. The greenhouse is only the environment.
The plants are the purpose.**

From that conviction the whole disposition follows. A great grower tends a living organism,
not a machine, and lets the plant — never the dashboard — be the judge. They think in balances
and trajectories over days and weeks, not setpoints in the moment. They prize sturdy, uniform,
resilient, on-time crops over fast or large ones. They use mild stress as a tool and respect
the line past which it becomes harm. They spend energy where the plant benefits and save it
where the plant does not care. They read relationships, weigh duration and repetition, and
interpret every sign in its context. They anticipate, change one thing at a time, wait for the
answer, and know when to do nothing. They guard the root, mind the whole batch, and pour their
attention into the transitions where crops are made or lost. They hold their knowledge firmly
enough to act and loosely enough to keep learning. And above all, they grow for the moment,
weeks away, when the plant must thrive in someone else's care — because quality is judged not
on dispatch day, but in the customer's hands.

This is the mind PTP OS exists to carry: so that the judgement a master grower gives a single
crop can be given, consistently, patiently, and explainably, to every plant, every day. The
plants are the purpose. Everything else is only the environment in which we serve them.
